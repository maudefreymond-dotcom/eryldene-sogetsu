The Place & History

A garden that has always been home to ikebana

Eryldene was conceived as a total work of art — house and garden inseparable, each an extension of the other. Built from 1913 in Gordon, Sydney, it is one of Australia's most significant heritage properties: the intact vision of architect William Hardy Wilson and Professor E.G. Waterhouse, whose 23-year collaboration produced a space where Eastern and Western traditions meet in perfect equilibrium.

"In his eighties, Professor Waterhouse began studying Japanese — to talk camellias with Japanese experts. His last book, written at 87, showed 78 camellias in 158 photographs — mostly in ikebana arrangements."

His wife, Janet Waterhouse, was the founding president of the Sydney Chapter of Ikebana International in 1959. A commemorative plaque at Eryldene still honours her. She was acknowledged as a world expert in the use of camellias in ikebana — flowers that still bloom in this garden today.

Norman Sparnon, who brought Sogetsu to Australia, co-authored the last book of E.G. Waterhouse — on camellias and ikebana. The man who introduced this art form to Australia wrote his final work with the man of this house. That thread runs directly into our June installation.

The Space

The former tennis court — 18 × 37 metres of possibility

This is not a white cube gallery. It is an outdoor space, enclosed by historic walls, shaded by canopy, held by stone and trellis. It breathes. It has seasons and memory. Our installations will not be placed in this space — they will be in conversation with it.

The court is divided into four zones, each with its own team and poetic territory. The Tea House sits at one end; the Moon Gate pierces the left wall; the Studio overlooks from the north. Every wall, every archway, every stone is both constraint and invitation.

The Creative Vision

Bringing the Court
Back to Life

This court has been silent for decades.

No more serves. No more volleys. No more chalk freshly drawn on a summer morning. The net is gone. The players have left. What remains is an empty rectangle of memory — 18 metres wide, 37 metres long, open to the sky.

We are here to bring it back. Through ikebana we will re-inhabit this court and give back to it what time has taken away. Branches, living material, and the poetic possibilities of a racket, a ball, a length of net: all are welcome here. The creative vocabulary is open.

Two Registers

Classic scale
meets land art

Classic-scale ikebana on stands — individual work inspired by the topic. Each practitioner brings their arrangement on the day, around a shared theme.

Large-scale ikebana inspired by land art — architectural, spatial. Created on site two days before the exhibition.

Sofu Teshigahara — the Picasso of Flowers — founded Sogetsu in 1927 on one principle: "Anyone can enjoy ikebana anytime, anywhere, using any material." This court is our proof.

Design Directions

Four Concepts

From that open vocabulary, four gestures emerge. Each one takes something that once belonged to this court — a movement, a boundary, a line, a moment — and gives it back through ikebana. Not illustration, but translation: the body of the game passing into the body of the arrangement.

I

The Arc of the Serve

The player reaches skyward — body arched, racket extended, the whole form suspended in one held breath. Arrangements capture that singular moment of expansion: lines rising and opening into space, tension gathered at the centre, intention made visible before the strike.

II

The Imaginary Net

That taut boundary between two players becomes a living structure. Branches caught mid-air. A rich surface for conceptual Sogetsu work: weaving, suspension, layering, void.

III

The Rebound

The ball strikes the court and springs back — a moment of pure energy. Arrangements that simulate that rebound: a downward plunge of line meeting the earth, then a sudden, liberated ascent. Tension held. Movement frozen.

IV

Out of Bounds

The line on the court is absolute — one side in, the other out. Arrangements playing with that boundary: materials crossing, spilling beyond an implied line. The ball that lands just beyond becomes the starting point for an exploration of limit, threshold.

Together these four acts of translation — serve, net, ball, line — are a reminiscence of the past. Each one takes something that once lived here.

Maps & Working Sketches

Four Zones · One Court

The court is divided into four installation zones, each with its own team and its own poetic territory. Each zone responds to the space, the theme, and the history — together they form a single coherent experience.

I
The Arc of the Serve
Arc zones · Front and back court

The player reaches skyward — body arched, form suspended in one held breath. Your arrangement captures that moment: lines rising, tension gathered at the centre, intention made visible before the strike.

II
The Imaginary Net
Front of Studio · Left side of court

The net that once divided this court becomes a living structure. Branches caught mid-air, a surface for weaving, suspension and void. The trellis structure echoes Wilson's bold lattice screen of 1936 — a 90-year-old precedent.

III
The Rebound
Court floor · Centre and left wall

The ball strikes the earth and springs back — a moment of pure energy. Your arrangement holds that tension: a downward plunge of line meeting the ground, then a sudden, liberated ascent. Movement frozen at the point of impact.

IV
Out of Bounds
Court perimeter · South-west boundary

The line on the court is absolute — one side in, the other out. Materials crossing, spilling beyond an implied boundary. The ball that lands just beyond becomes a starting point: an exploration of limit, threshold, and what lies at the edge.
